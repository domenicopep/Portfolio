01 — Motivation

Why build a signal engine from scratch

Most retail quantitative strategies rely on momentum or mean-reversion as primary signals. These are well-understood, well-arbitraged, and fragile in volatile regimes. The goal was to build a system that could ingest a wider set of factors — including fundamental data from SEC filings — and weight them dynamically based on posterior probability rather than static allocations.

C++ was chosen over Python for its deterministic memory management and throughput requirements when parsing streaming data across multiple instruments simultaneously.

02 — Architecture

Data ingestion and factor extraction

The engine parses two primary data streams: live market data (price, volume, order book) and SEC EDGAR filings (10-K and 10-Q reports). From these, it extracts 25 financial factors spanning momentum, value, quality, and sentiment categories.

The parser handles both structured market feeds and unstructured filing text. Factor values are normalized across a rolling lookback window to maintain comparability across instruments and time periods.

  • Live market data parsing (price, volume, order flow)
  • SEC EDGAR 10-K/10-Q filing extraction
  • 25 factors across momentum, value, quality, sentiment
  • Rolling normalization for cross-sectional comparability
03 — Inference

Bayesian factor weighting

Rather than fixed factor weights, the engine uses Bayesian inference with Monte Carlo sampling to estimate posterior return distributions for each instrument. Factor weights are updated as new data arrives, with the prior shaped by historical regime performance.

This means the system naturally downweights momentum signals during high-volatility regimes and upweights quality factors — without explicit regime-switching logic. The posterior is estimated through Markov Chain Monte Carlo sampling with convergence diagnostics.

04 — Backtesting

Validation and results

Multi-year backtesting with Sharpe ratio optimization was conducted against a baseline momentum strategy. The Bayesian approach consistently produced superior risk-adjusted returns, particularly during periods of regime transition where static-weight models underperform.
