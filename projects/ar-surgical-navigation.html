01 — Context

The problem with surgical navigation

Fracture reduction procedures rely heavily on intraoperative fluoroscopy — repeated x-ray imaging to verify bone alignment. This exposes both patient and surgical team to cumulative radiation. The core challenge: how do you give surgeons spatial awareness of their instruments relative to bone fragments without constant imaging.

At the Surreality Lab, the approach was to overlay preoperative CT plans onto the surgical field using augmented reality, anchored by real-time optical tracking.

02 — Approach

Optical tracking and coordinate transforms

The system interfaces with NDI optical and electromagnetic tracking hardware for real-time 6DOF (six degrees of freedom) position and orientation data. The tracking pipeline handles binary protocol parsing of BX and BX2 data frames, quaternion-based coordinate transformations between tracker space and patient anatomy, and streaming data modes for high-frequency applications.

Registration maps the preoperative CT coordinate system onto the physical patient using fiducial markers. The resulting transformation matrix enables AR overlays to align with sub-millimeter tolerance.

  • NDI Polaris Lyra optical tracking at 60Hz
  • Binary protocol parsing (BX/BX2 frames)
  • Quaternion-based coordinate transformations
  • Fiducial-based patient registration
  • Real-time streaming data modes

03 — ML Integration

Translating 3D data into implant specifications

Beyond navigation, the lab developed ML algorithms to translate 3D operative augmented reality data into precise rod specifications for spinal surgery. This enables surgeons to validate implant geometries through dry fitting before physical contouring — reducing operative time and improving fit accuracy.

The algorithm takes the tracked 3D positions of pedicle screws, computes optimal rod curvature through a constrained optimization, and outputs specifications that can be verified in AR before bending the physical rod.

04 — Outcomes

Results and clinical validation

The system was validated in collaboration with orthopedic surgeons and neurosurgeons against clinical requirements. Iterative design improvements were driven by surgical workflow analysis across both the navigation and rod-planning projects.
