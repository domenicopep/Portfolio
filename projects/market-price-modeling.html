01 — Problem

Dynamic pricing as a regression problem

Ride-hailing surge pricing is driven by local supply/demand imbalances that shift rapidly. A static linear model misses these dynamics entirely. The question was whether multivariate regression with carefully engineered interaction terms could capture the nonlinear pricing behavior that emerges during regime shifts — peak hours, weather events, venue surges.

The dataset consisted of 10,000+ Uber ride records with temporal, geographic, and demand-side features.

[fig. 3.1] Surge pricing distribution across demand regimes
02 — Model Development

Feature engineering and interaction terms

The base model used 3 predictors and achieved a reasonable but insufficient fit. The insight was that pricing regimes interact — time of day modulates the effect of demand density differently during weekdays versus weekends, and distance interacts with surge multiplier nonlinearly.

Linear interaction and cross-term features were engineered to capture these local regime shifts. Multiple partial F-tests were performed to determine which interaction slopes contributed significant explanatory power, systematically testing nested models against expanded alternatives.

  • 3 primary predictors with engineered interactions
  • Multiple partial F-test for interaction significance
  • Cross-term features for regime shift capture
  • Nested model comparison at each stage
03 — Validation

Cross-validation and diagnostics

Model robustness was validated through k-fold cross-validation to guard against overfitting — especially important given the interaction terms which add model complexity. Residual diagnostics confirmed homoscedasticity and normality assumptions held within acceptable bounds.

The engineered features improved predictive accuracy from 65% to 87%, with the R² reaching 0.78 — a substantial improvement for a dataset with inherent pricing stochasticity.
